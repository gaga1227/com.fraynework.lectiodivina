“How I would like a poor Church for the poor!” - The Rise and Demise of Catholic Education?

Empirical research, (Census, NCIC, My Schools Website) identifies a generational trend indicating that two thirds of children who attend Australian Catholic schools are from the middle and upper class socio-economic strata. In contrast many of the poor, marginalized and indigenous go elsewhere. Not surprisingly for many, Catholic education is offering a preferential option for the wealthy, where the wants of the comfortable appear to be addressed before the needs of the poor (Crittenden, 2008; Fitzgerald, 2009; Wilkinson, 2011), Again, sadly for some: “Catholic secondary schools have absorbed an ethos which is pragmatic, competitive, consumerist, and materialist” (Collins, 2008).

This presentation:

  • analyses the research concerning who are and who are not in Australian Catholic schools;
  • offers a rationale of mission for Catholic schools using the Vatican’s Catholic school in the Third Millennium (1998); and O’Murchu’s (2011) Christianity’s dangerous memory ;
  • critiques initiatives used by Catholic schools to re-engage with a preferential option for the poor.

Associate Professor Denis McLaughlin, Educational Leadership, Australian Catholic University; Dr Phil Standen, Senior Lecturer Education, Australian Catholic University

No Ranking for Social Justice? Measuring Quality Outcomes in Mauritian Schools.

This paper aims at exploring the current conventions of quality in Mauritian and Australian Catholic schools and puts forward the need to establish alternative quality standards inspired from social justice principles.

Mrs. Lily-Claire Deenmamode, Research Student, Australian Catholic University; Dr. Deborah Robertson, Director Educational Leadership, Australian Catholic University
